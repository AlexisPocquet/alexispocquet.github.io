Research Experience

  • M1 Research Internship Université Paris-Saclay, LMF, Deducteam team · Thibaut Benjamin · march 2026 to july 2026

    Over my last M1 semester, I worked with the Deducteam at LMF (Université Paris-Saclay) under the supervision of Thibaut Benjamin, on the type-theoretical foundations of higher-dimensional algebra. Weak ω-categories are higher-dimensional algebraic structures generalizing the geometry of types in intentional type theory, and are a leading candidate for representing homotopy types — though many constructions within this representation remain poorly understood. The goal of this internship is to extend a type-theoretical presentation of weak ω-categories with parametricity axioms, corresponding to a class of cylinder construction operations.

  • L3 Research Work CNRS, ENS Paris, Antique team · Jérôme Féret and Jérôme Boillot · january 2025 to july 2025

    Over my last L3 semester, I worked with the Antique team (CNRS, ENS Ulm) on Abstract Interpretation and its applications to system software verification. My semester project studied the reduced product construction for combining abstract domains. My internship extended this towards OS verification on IA-32: I introduced an intermediate language with a formal semantics for assembly instructions, derived a reachability semantics over memory states, and constructed a sound over-approximation stable under task execution — provided applications do not compromise the host system's critical data structures. [more] Abstract Interpretation, introduced by Radhia and Patrick Cousot in the late 1970s, formalizes program analysis as computations over over-approximations of reachable states — ensuring termination while retaining enough precision to infer meaningful invariants. The reduced product is a general construction for combining abstract domains; my semester work studied several instantiations, including those based on input/output channels and ghost quantities.

Programming

  • Language Pipelines 2024 — 2026

    Two compiler projects tracing a clear progression. KawaLang (Université Paris-Saclay, winter 2024) is a first contact with PLT tooling: a sequential interpreter for an OO toy language where name resolution drives both type-checking and evaluation. MiniGoLang (École Polytechnique, winter 2026) reflects a more disciplined approach — errors are accumulated monadically across the full front-end, and a typed-AST rewriting pass precedes x86-64 code generation. [more] The jump between the two projects is the real story: from a pipeline that fails eagerly on the first error, to one that accumulates diagnostics via a dedicated Error module and separates concerns between the rewriting and emission phases. The compiler passes all provided test cases, including nested struct access, forward calls, and mutually recursive functions. See the implementation report for details.

  • TinyProver fall 2025

    An interactive proof assistant developed for the computational logic course at École Polytechnique, implementing two independent provers in OCaml: one for propositional logic via STLC, one for a minimal kernel of dependent type theory. [more] The STLC prover supports bidirectional type checking and an interactive tactic mode with sequent manipulation. The dependent types kernel implements the core of Martin-Löf type theory: substitution, parallel beta-reduction, alpha-conversion, and bidirectional inference for Pi-types, natural numbers, and propositional equality. The hardest part was normalization: a naive fixpoint fails on terms defined by recursion over Nat, requiring a more careful reduction strategy. The project ends with a reflection on the gap between a minimal kernel and a usable proof assistant.

  • MiniRust spring 2025

    A partial implementation of Rust's ownership discipline, developed for the advanced functional programming course at ENS Paris-Saclay. The pipeline runs a type checker and a borrow checker before emitting C. [more] The borrow checker handles the common cases but does not cover all edge cases — the interesting challenge was deciding where to draw the line and document the gaps honestly. On the backend, the most technically involved part is struct dependency resolution: a postfix DFS over a dependency graph ensures definitions are emitted in the right order, with cyclic dependencies through indirection handled implicitly. 91 of 94 tests pass.
